1.1 The Oral Cavity (ÿßŸÑŸÅŸÖ)

The oral cavity extends from the oral fissure (lips) anteriorly to the oropharyngeal isthmus (opening to oropharynx) posteriorly. It is divided by the dental arches into the Vestibule and the Mouth Cavity Proper.

The Vestibule

  • Location: Space between lips/cheeks (externally) and gums/teeth (internally).
  • Lateral Wall: Formed by the Buccinator muscle.
  • Clinical Note: The parotid salivary gland duct opens opposite the upper 2nd molar tooth.

The Mouth Proper

  • Roof: Hard palate (anterior) and Soft palate (posterior).
  • Floor: Anterior 2/3 of the tongue, Mylohyoid muscle, Sublingual gland, and Submandibular gland.

The Palate (Roof)

1. Hard Palate: Bony anterior part. Formed by the maxilla and palatine bones.

2. Soft Palate: Mobile, muscular posterior flap, ending in the uvula.

The Tongue (ÿßŸÑŸÑÿ≥ÿßŸÜ)

A muscular structure covered by mucous membrane.

Parts & Surfaces
  • Division: Divided by the V-shaped Sulcus Terminalis into Anterior 2/3 (Oral part) and Posterior 1/3 (Pharyngeal part).
  • Foramen Cecum: A pit at the apex of the sulcus, remnant of the thyroglossal duct.
  • Dorsum (Anterior 2/3): Covered in papillae:
    • Filiform: Cone-shaped, no taste buds.
    • Fungiform: Round, has taste buds.
    • Vallate: 8-12 large, V-shaped, has taste buds.
  • Dorsum (Posterior 1/3): No papillae. Contains the Lingual Tonsil.
  • Inferior Surface: Thin mucosa, shows deep lingual veins. Connected to the floor by the Frenulum Linguae.
Muscles of the Tongue

All supplied by Hypoglossal N. (CN XII) except Palatoglossus.

  • Intrinsic (Change Shape): Superior/Inferior longitudinal, Transverse, Vertical.
  • Extrinsic (Produce Movement): Genioglossus (protrudes), Hyoglossus (depresses), Styloglossus (retracts/elevates), Palatoglossus (elevates posterior tongue; Nerve: Pharyngeal Plexus - CN X).

1.2 The Pharynx (ÿßŸÑÿ®ŸÑÿπŸàŸÖ)

A 25cm fibromuscular tube for air and food.

Wall & Muscles

  • Wall Layers (Inside-Out): Mucosa $\rightarrow$ Pharyngobasilar Fascia $\rightarrow$ Muscles $\rightarrow$ Buccopharyngeal Fascia.
  • Muscles (Outer Circular Constrictors): Superior, Middle, Inferior.
  • Muscles (Inner Longitudinal Elevators): Palatopharyngeus, Salpingopharyngeus, Stylopharyngeus.

Divisions of the Pharynx

  1. Nasopharynx: (Base of skull to soft palate).
    Features: Pharyngeal Tonsil (Adenoids) and Eustachian Tube Opening.
  2. Oropharynx: (Soft palate to epiglottis).
    Features: Palatine Tonsils (in tonsillar fossa) and Lingual Tonsil.
  3. Laryngopharynx: (Epiglottis to cricoid cartilage C6).
    Features: Laryngeal inlet and Piriform Fossae.

Neurovascular Supply of the Pharynx

  • Motor Nerves: All muscles by Vagus N. (CN X) EXCEPT Stylopharyngeus (by Glossopharyngeal N. - CN IX).
  • Sensory Nerves: Pharyngeal Plexus (formed by CN IX for sensory, CN X for motor).
1.3 The Esophagus (ÿßŸÑŸÖÿ±Ÿäÿ°)

A 25cm fibromuscular tube from pharynx to stomach.

  • Course: Begins at C6 (cricoid cartilage), passes through diaphragm at T10 (esophageal hiatus), ends at T11 (cardiac orifice).
  • Parts: Cervical (in neck), Thoracic (in mediastinum), Abdominal (short, ~1.25 cm).
  • Key Relations: Posterior to trachea, posterior to the left atrium.

Esophageal Constrictions

Four key anatomical narrowing points.

Constriction Location Vertebral Level
1st (Narrowest) Pharyngoesophageal junction C6
2nd Crossed by Arch of Aorta T3
3rd Crossed by Left Main Bronchus T6
4th Esophageal Hiatus (Diaphragm) T10

Neurovascular Supply (Segmental)

  • Arterial: Cervical (Inferior Thyroid a.), Thoracic (Thoracic Aorta), Abdominal (Left Gastric a.).
  • Venous: Cervical (Inferior Thyroid v.), Thoracic (Azygos & Hemiazygos v.), Abdominal (Left Gastric v. [Portal] & Hemiazygos v. [Systemic]).
  • Clinical (Porto-Systemic Anastomosis): The abdominal esophageal veins connect the portal and systemic systems. In liver disease (portal hypertension), blood backs up into the Left Gastric Vein, causing esophageal varices.
  • Nerve: Esophageal Plexus (Vagus + Sympathetic). Forms Anterior (Left) Vagal Trunk and Posterior (Right) Vagal Trunk.
1.4 The Stomach (ÿßŸÑŸÖÿπÿØÿ©)

The widest part of the alimentary tract, acting as a food reservoir.

External Features & Parts

  • Openings: Cardiac orifice (esophagus), Pyloric orifice (duodenum).
  • Curvatures: Lesser (right border) and Greater (left border).
  • Parts: Fundus (dome), Body (main part), Pylorus (pyloric antrum + canal).

Openings & Sphincters

  1. Cardiac Orifice: A physiologic sphincter (no true muscle). Reflux is prevented by the diaphragmatic crus, acute angle, and mucosal folds.
  2. Pyloric Orifice: A true anatomic and physiologic sphincter (thickened circular muscle).

Wall of the Stomach

  • Mucosa: Has folds called Rugae when empty, which flatten when distended.
  • Musculosa: Unique 3 layers: Outer Longitudinal, Middle Circular, Inner Oblique.

Relations of the Stomach

  • Anterior: Left lobe of liver, diaphragm, anterior abdominal wall.
  • Posterior (The "Stomach Bed"): Structures the stomach rests on (separated by lesser sac):
    • Vertical: Diaphragm, Spleen, Left Suprarenal Gland, Left Kidney.
    • Horizontal: Splenic Artery, Pancreas, Transverse Mesocolon, Transverse Colon.

Arterial Supply (from Celiac Trunk)

The stomach has a rich, anastomotic blood supply.

Artery Origin Supplies
Left Gastric a. Celiac Trunk Upper Lesser Curvature
Right Gastric a. Hepatic a. Lower Lesser Curvature
Short Gastric aa. Splenic a. Fundus
Left Gastroepiploic a. Splenic a. Upper Greater Curvature
Right Gastroepiploic a. Gastroduodenal a. Lower Greater Curvature

Venous & Lymphatic Drainage

  • Veins: All drain to the Portal Circulation (Portal, Splenic, or Superior Mesenteric Veins).
  • Lymph: All lymph ultimately drains to the Celiac Nodes.

Applying anatomical knowledge to clinical reasoning.

Case 1: The Deviated Tongue (CN XII Lesion)

Presentation: Patient's tongue deviates to the right upon protrusion after right-sided neck surgery.

Anatomy: Protrusion is a function of the Genioglossus muscle, innervated by the Hypoglossal N. (CN XII).

Reasoning: The right CN XII was damaged. The healthy left Genioglossus pushes the tongue unopposed, causing deviation TOWARDS the paralyzed side.

Learning Point: Tongue deviates TOWARD the side of a CN XII lesion.

Case 2: Nasal Regurgitation (CN X Lesion)

Presentation: Patient reports water coming from the nose when drinking; uvula deviates to the left.

Anatomy: The Levator Veli Palatini (motor nerve: Vagus N. - CN X) elevates the soft palate to seal the nasopharynx during swallowing.

Reasoning: A right-sided Vagus nerve lesion paralyzes the right soft palate. The healthy left side pulls the uvula AWAY from the lesion. The failed seal causes nasal regurgitation.

Learning Point: Uvula deviates AWAY from the side of a CN X lesion.

Case 3: The Lodged Coin (Esophageal Constrictions)

Presentation: A child swallows a coin and experiences acute dysphagia (difficulty swallowing).

Anatomy: Foreign bodies lodge at the 4 anatomical constrictions. The narrowest point is the 1st constriction (pharyngoesophageal junction) at C6.

Learning Point: The anatomical constrictions (C6, T3, T6, T10) are clinically significant sites for impaction.

Case 4: The Penetrating Ulcer (Stomach Bed)

Presentation: Patient with a known gastric ulcer develops new, severe pain "boring" straight to his back.

Anatomy: The posterior wall of the stomach ("Stomach Bed") is directly anterior to the Pancreas and the Splenic Artery.

Reasoning: The ulcer has likely penetrated the posterior stomach wall and is irritating/eroding the Pancreas (causing the referred back pain).

Major Risk: Erosion into the Splenic Artery, causing massive, life-threatening hemorrhage.

Learning Point: The stomach's posterior relations (Pancreas, Splenic Artery) are critical in peptic ulcer disease.
